Spatial Data

What is Spatial Data

Spatial data is any type of data that directly or indirectly references a specific geographical area or location.

Geographical data strictly refers to data that identifies geographical locations.

Example: Open Street Map API

SDBMS vs GIS

SDBMS allows GIS to scale to large databases.

Spatial Database Management Systems (SDBMS)

  • Handles a large amount of spatial data in secondary storage.
  • Spatial semantics are built into the query language.
  • Specialized index structure to access spatial data.

Geographical Information Systems (GIS)

  • Are typically SDBMS clients.
  • Has a rich set of geographical analysis features.
  • Information is typically organized in layers: transportation, water bodies, suburbs, etc.

Types of Spatial Data

Point Data

  • Geolocation of some entities in space
  • Point data as a time series: trajectory data
  • Raster lines in satellite images

Region Data

Data of objects with spatial information with location and boundary are usually represented by polygons.

Models of Spatial Information

Two common models are:

  • Space-based: maps spatial data to objects with other attributes.
  • Object-based: spatial data as an attribute of objects.

Coordinate Systems

Each instance of a spatial type has a spatial reference identifier (SRID). It is a unique identifier associated with a specific coordinate system, tolerance, and resolution.

There are several types of coordinate systems:

  • Cartesian corrdinates
  • Geodetic or geocentric coordinates (longitude and latitude)
  • Projected coordinates: obtained by performing a mathematical mapping from a geo-coordinate to a Cartesian one.

For example, the Mercator projection (shown right) tries to transform the surface of a sphere into a rectangular map. This process introduces distortion, becoming more extreme the further from the equator.

WGS84 vs Australian GDA94

The World Geodetic System 1984 (WGS84) is the spatial reference system of GPS satellites with an error of fewer than 2 centimeters to the center of Earth.

The official geodetic datum for Australia is the Geocentric Datum of Australia 1994 (GDA94).

The spheroids used for WGS84 and GDA84 are also almost identical, and both systems are geocentric. Thus for most mapping, exploration, and GIS users, WGS84 and GDA94 coordinates will be the same. [...] For precise surveys, however, the difference between WGS84 and GDA94 may be significant and changes slowly over time. [...] The difference between GDA94 and WGS84 is approximately 45cm in 2000.

Spatial Queries

PostgreSQL supports spatial data out of the box.

  • Geometric types such as point, line, box, path, polygon, circle
  • Geometric operations (functions on the geometric types) such as intersection, distance, etc.
  • Geometric indices with CREATE INDEX <idx_name> ON <table> USING GIST(<geomatric_col>);

There is also the PostGIS extension with:

  • More geometry types and geographic types
  • Support for spatial reference systems
  • Spatial predicates using the nine-intersection model
  • More spatial operators
  • R-tree indexing

Topological Relationships

Topological relationships are invariant under elastic deformation (without a tear or merge). For example, two shapes that touch each other in a Cartesian plane should also touch in a spherical system. Topology is the study of such relationships.

Spatial Joins

Spatial queries can be much more computationally heavy than non-spatial queries. Typically, the database adopts a two-step filter-refine strategy for spatial data join operations.

  • Filter - find shapes with their minimum bounding boxes intersecting the target regions;
  • Refine - select shapes that truly intersect with the target regions from the results of the previous step.

Indexing of Spatial Data

For one-dimensional data (points), we use a classic B+ Tree.

For n-dimensional data, we use a spatial R+ Tree.

R+ Tree

The R-tree is a tree-structured index that remains balanced on insertions and deletions.

Each key stored in a leaf entry is intuitively a box, or collection of intervals, with one interval per dimension.
